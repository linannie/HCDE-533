Final Project - Clock Decoration


AKA why did I choose such a hard project


Concept

For my final project I made a clock decoration. Initially I was thinking about making the mechanical portion, but I decided to make it a wall decoration because I feel like I needed more time to make the weight and pendulum portion more accurate. To design the clock decoration, I learned about gear ratios and compound gears, since the mechanism of the hour, minute, and second hand rotations depend on each other through gears.


Techniques Used:
  • Laser cutting - gears, base frame, minute hand
  • Rhino - to model the gears and parts
  • Grasshopper - to model the gears through parametric design
  • 3D printing - printed wall mounts and dowels

The parts used to build the clock or decor:
  • Gear Train
  • Escapement Mechanism
  • Weight + Pendulum
  • Base to attach everything to
  • Minute hand
  • Dowels
  • Wall mounts
  • Rubber bands, glue


Technical Implementation

Step 1 - Calculating Gear Ratios

For the gear ratios, I used 60:1 gear ratio of the minute hand (1 full rotation for 60 minutes) and a 12:1 gear ratio for the hour (1 full rotation for 12 hours). In the end, I ended up just using the minute hand ratio and got rid of the hour hand. The 60:1 gear ratio is pretty big, so I broke up the ratio to 2:1, 5:1, and 6:1, and I left the 12:1 gear ratio as is.


Gear Train Possibilities

I developed 2 possible sketches depending on the 3D orientation of my clock/mechanism. (This sketch isn’t showing the final 3D orientation.) I would have 8 or 9 gears with teeth based on the gear ratios. In the first sketch, I have 9 gears because the minute and hour hand are separate. In the second sketch the minute and hour gears are co-axial and the total number of gears is 8. The sketches also show the number of teeth each gear would have.

Step 2 - Surveying Parts at Ace Hardware

Before I designed anything, I needed to look at a few parts so I could design my gears based on that. The part I was looking at was the axel for the gears, so I could make a hole in the gear design based on the diameter of the axel. I found a dowel about 3/8” in diameter that I decided to use on the center of my gears

I also got some scrap wood from the store, which I measured and used for my test cuts. The thickness was about 6.2mm.

Step 3 - Designing the gears in Rhino

For this step I accomplished:
- Rhino modeling ✅
- Grasshopper parametric design ✅

For this part, I had to understand the parts of a gear like the pitch and base diameter. Luckily there were a few plugins that I found on Google that I could use with Rhino to help me model my gears (but I had to model the escapement gear and small gears myself). I downloaded a Python a plugin geargen to design my gears. It took inputs such as the Base Diameter and number of teeth to create a 2D curve of my gear, and this gave me an output of the outline of a gear.



I had to cut out the center for the dowel, and cut out parts of the shape so it wouldn’t be completely solid. Using the plugin I designed gears B, D, and F.


Parametric Design

Unfortunately, the plugin I used had a minimum input for the number (17 teeth) of teeth on a gear, and the smallest gear from my design had 9 teeth, so I decided to model it myself. I created a curve shape that looked like a tooth on a gear. Using grasshopper, I revolved the curve around a Base Diameter, I bake the circle and the revolved curves and Boolean Unioned to create a gear shape. Similarly, I did this for the escapement gear except the teeth were triangles.



For the escapement gear, I used the Grasshopper similarly but used a triangular curve to make the teeth.

Dowels

I went back to the hardware store to test the dowels and I found the aluminum dowel that fit my gears!

However, they were sold out of the wooden dowel in the size I wanted, so I decided to 3D print my own. My 3D printed dowels were 3/8” in diameter and 7cm long.



Step 5 - Designing a Mounting System

After I cut all the gears out and printed the dowels, I had to assemble everything together somehow. I hadn’t planned out a base frame at this point, so I sketched a few ideas. I knew I had to attach the dowels to the same plane so the gears that touched would be in the right spot. I laid paper down and traced the placement of each dowel based on the correct orientation of the gear train I wanted and measured the distance in between each dowel. (I’m sure there are ways to calculate the distance between gears, but I didn’t feel like doing math.) The distance between the axels were: 33mm, 71mm, and 90mm.


I went back to the MILL to cut a base rectangle to attach my dowels to and I designed a wall mount for my dowels, which I ended up 3D printing.

Problem 1: I realized a few of my gears (escapement and hour gear train)would actually coincide with the dowels. I could’ve made a bigger escapement wheel or a different ratios.

Fix: Based on this and how much time I had left, I decided to get rid of the hour gear train completely and orient the escapement wheel differently than I had planned.


Step 6 - Assembly and Troubleshooting

Here’s the part that took me the most time!

Glueing

First I glued the gears to the dowels so they would be secure and not spin freely. I put the gears in place on my base frame and tested the wall mounts. Right away there were a few problems.

Problem 2: I didn’t account for the weights of the gears, so some of the gears drooped while they were in the mounts.

Fix: Double up on wall mounts. I added another wall mount on top of the first one, so it would alleviate the drooping.

Problem 3: While manual turning the gears, I noticed some of the gears were shifting back and forth and wobbling.

Fix: I used rubber bands to stop the shifting and made sure there was adequate spacing in between gears so they wouldn't have too much friction.

Final Result

Here is the final wall decoration I created! It's a pretty cool wall piece, and if you move it manually, you can still see the gears move with each other. The timekeeping portion didn't work out so I ended up with a cool wall decoration, which didn't deviate too much from the original design.




Extra Step I Tried - Weight and Pendulum

This was the portion where I decided to get rid of the automatic timekeeping portion completely because I didn't have enough time to calculate the length and weight used. I still tried out an arbitraty numbers for the weight and pendulum but found out I had too much fricition between the teeth of my gears. I used a bag of rice as the weight and laser cut the pendulum peices (glued them together)

I tested the weight on the escapement gear, which worked well.

However, when I put the weight on the final gear train, my gears had too much friction with each other, so even with the weight, the escapement mechanism wouldn't run smoothly. If I had to fabricate the gears again, I would 3D print the gear train because PLA filament would likely produce less friction.
